Where We Dive

Three sites. Three entirely different oceans.

Each dive site on this list rewards a different kind of attention — different topography, different marine life, different quality of light.

30–70 ft

Maui

Molokini Crater

A crescent of submerged volcanic caldera rising from open ocean, twelve miles off Maui's south coast. The sheltered interior holds visibility past 100 feet on calm mornings, with dense schools of pyramid butterflyfish moving like smoke along the curved wall.

25–45 ft

Oahu

Turtle Canyon

A coral plateau two miles off Waikiki where Hawaiian green sea turtles — honu — rest on the reef shelf and occasionally approach divers with what can only be described as indifference. The shallow depth makes it accessible to open-water certified divers and advanced snorkelers alike.

Waikiki's resident honu colony since the 1970s View site details
40–70 ft

Lanai

Cathedrals at Lanai

Two interconnected lava tube chambers off Hulopoe Bay, where sunlight enters through openings in the roof and tracks across the chamber floor in narrow amber shafts around midday. Spinner dolphins pass the entrance on the return ascent often enough that your guide will factor it into the briefing.

The Charter

What a private dive looks like

Every charter is built around your certification level, your interests, and the conditions on the day. There is no fixed itinerary handed to a group of twelve. There is a boat, a guide, and the four of you.

  1. 1

    Pre-dive briefing with your guide

    Your guide reviews the site topography, notes what marine life is active at this depth and season, covers hand signals, and confirms your comfort with the planned profile. No jargon — this is a conversation, not a lecture.

  2. 2

    Kitted up and in the water within minutes of anchoring

    Equipment is pre-configured to your measurements before you board. At the site, the guide handles weight checks and gear inspection. Entry is when the conditions are right — not when the schedule says so.

  3. 3

    50–70 minute guided dive; the guide points out everything

    Your guide leads at a pace set by the least experienced diver. They will tap the tank, point at the reef, and hold position while you watch. The frogfish camouflaged on the encrusting coral, the juvenile spotted eagle ray in the sand channel — this is what the private format is for.

  4. 4

    Topside debrief, photos shared same day

    Back on the boat, the guide walks through what you saw and answers questions about the species and geology. Underwater photos taken by the guide are edited and delivered to your phone before dinner.
